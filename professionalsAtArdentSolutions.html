What professionals will I engage with before owning land in Meru?





Read on to know who they are and what they do.

Urban Planner

An urban planner is a person who creates plans and programs for the use of land in towns, cities, counties, and metropolitan areas. Planning is used to build communities, accommodate growth, or revitalize physical infrastructure.
To construct, grow, or rejuvenate a town or area, urban planners analyze the needs of the community and create short and long-term plans. To make sure that projected facilities, like schools, would be able to accommodate a changing population, they might, for instance, look at the plans for those facilities. Planners assist communities in managing the connected economic, social, and environmental challenges when a region develops or changes, such as creating a new park, providing shelter for the homeless, or enhancing the region's business climate.

Role of a planner in the construction process.

Let’s discuss the role of a planner in building. A planner stands at the focal point of any construction; For starters, it is a planner that designates the various land uses. It's a common practice in Kenya, planning consultancy firms are commissioned to undertake a land use plan or master planning for a site. It could be a county special plan, it could be an integrated strategic urban development plan, or any plan by the county or the national government. We have also seen counties do the special plans by themselves, those that have adequate human capacity. Essentially, a planner designates the land uses.

Now looking at specific sites during construction; it is a planner then that is commissioned by a developer once the developer finds it suitable to change the use of a land from one what it is in the title deed or in the lease conditions, they will commission a registered physical planner to undertake a change of use. The commissioned registered physical planner then looks at the site and scopes it for appropriateness, then if it is in line or aligned to the zoning requirements they can then advise the client to go ahead with the change of use because the site is aligned to the zoning guidelines. Thereafter, the planner on behalf of the property owner or the developer putting an application for the change of use to the planning authority, the application goes through online systems sometimes, there are a number of counties that have a development application management systems that is online, in other counties you’ll make the application as a physically. Thereafter, the planning authority, mostly the sub-county planner, will assess the application and decide whether or not to approve the change of use.

Once a development permission is granted for a change of use, then the planner registers the new land use to the relevant land registry. Other applications that physical planner undertakes on behalf of a developer include subdivision schemes, extension of use, extension of leases, amalgamation etc. In a nutshell, any person that undertakes any material change on the use of a structure or any works on land, needs to engage the services of a registered physical planner.

 Construction Project Manager

A Construction Project Manager basically in charge of making sure that the contractor is doing things as scoped in the bills of quantities, in the architect's drawings, in the structural engineers drawings, the mechanical, electrical engineers drawings. He/she is the key person in ensuring the contractor does not go above and beyond that scope, ensuring that the workmanship delivered on site is as specified and pointing out issues that may arise correctly or in future.

The manager can always project based on his/her experience and analysis of the drawings that they are presented with. They interpret textual drawings and all engineering drawings and convey to a contractor, and the consultants any likely challenge, or point to a already existing challenge so as to mitigate the effects of the challenges.

From a government perspective, the project manager’s job is to envision an entire project from inception to completion. The manager, together with his team sits down and decide what is required. Eg, do they require to have additional investment in a new project or refurbish an existing building to increase its rental value, to increase its appearance, to mitigate against very expensive repairs etc. Construction project delivery and planning are supervised by project managers. They make sure that the work is finished on schedule and on budget. They plan logistics, assign tasks, and monitor expenses.

The Land Control Board.

Great! You have identified your land, you have identified the best lawyer to work with, you've done your due diligence, you've paid your deposit and your transaction is well underway. Now you have to deal with the land control board in order to finish properly, and in case you don't know, the land control board or typically referred to as LCBs as a acronym, are oversight bodies created by the Ministry of Lands and Planning to oversee land transactions, primarily that are agricultural. That's a very important thing to remember if you're buying land in urban centers like Nairobi and Mombasa, property that is typically leasehold, residential or commercial, you'll never need to deal with the Land Control Board. However, if you're buying agricultural land which is typically freehold, as most people who do land banking or invest in land, you will have to deal with the Land Control Board if you're going to engage in a clean, legal and morally sound transaction.

The LCBs exist in every county. Every single one of the 47 counties should have a land control board and they typically come into your transaction when you're seeking permission to subdivide a piece of property, or if your seller needs to subdivide his land to sell your portion, or if you're looking to subdivide your own piece of land. They will also need to give you permission to transfer that piece of property from the seller to yourself or vice versa.

You will also need to get consent from the land control board when you're trying to change the particulars on a title deed. Eg, if there's a spelling error on a title deed, maybe the name wasn't spelled correctly or the address wasn't referenced properly, or any kind of error on a title deed that needs correcting, you have to get a Land Control Board's permission to make that correction. The LCBs are very critical, and an integral part of land transactions in this country.

Sadly very many people circumvent this process because it can be inconvenient sometimes. It's time-consuming and uncomfortable because you have to present yourself to this grouping of people in the particular jurisdiction that you're buying land, and explain your case. They can either agree to give you the permission that you're seeking or they can deny it and that will have major implications in your ability to close the transaction.
Who sits in a Land Control Board? The Land Control Boards are typically chaired by the County Commissioners in each county or their deputies, they also have a secretary who is a representative from the Ministry of lands in that particular area, a representative from the planning department or the survey department and the Ministry of lands, representatives from NEMA, and most importantly or equally as important they will have representation from the actual community.

The primary role of the land control board is to control the activities around land, whether it's subdivisions of fragmentation or transfer from one party to another, and in some cases even amalgamations, when you're trying to join small pieces of of land into one giant mass. Sometimes the land control will need to get involved because these activities can have major implications on the community in which the land sits.

Most Land Control Boards will meet once a month. They usually have an announcement board where they will have their schedule hopefully displayed for the general public to know when they will meet in advance.

Surveyor

What's the role of a surveyor when purchasing land? This is a very important person in the whole chain of events. And this is why; Typically when you're buying land you'll be shown a particular parcel of land somewhere that you might like and want to proceed with. You'll be told what the size is that you need to pay for, and also the title deed will stipulate what the size of that land is.
What a lot of people may not realize is they're often very huge discrepancies between the size of a property on the ground versus what it says on a on a document like the title deed. This is where the surveyor comes in.

You must have a reliable and a professional surveyor to work with who can confirm not only the size of the piece of property that you're buying but also its location. This might seem trivial but it's an important thing to note. It's not uncommon for you to be shown a particular piece of property that's actually not where it's supposed to be. There have been cases where we’ve scouted for land, and upon doing a due diligence which includes working with a surveyor to confirm the dimensions and location of the property, turned out that the property we were actually going to pay for based on the title deed documents was almost two kilometers away from the one that we were shown. There's no way for you to know whether or not that location is accurate if you're new to the area, so it's important for you to work with a surveyor to confirm its location. The first thing you wanna do is to make sure that they are a licensed surveyor.

The second thing you want to make sure you do is that you understand that they are local. While there are surveyors or survey companies that will do work anywhere in the country, it always makes the process go a lot faster when you pick one who's familiar with the local area that you're buying the property. The reason for that is because they will understand things about the community, and the history of the land.
The person you pick, you’ll be working with them for some time, you need to have good rapport, they need to be patient with you as you ask questions. They need to be willing to go the extra mile to make sure that you are comfortable. They're working for you so you need to make sure that they are likable. The other thing you need to do again is make sure that they are of good integrity. You can never underestimate or talk too much about the importance of integrity. It helps to ask around. Ask reputable people and they'll lead you to the right one.

Why is integrity important? The business of land acquisition is riddled with cons, best to do your due diligence. If your antennas are tuned correctly, these are things you can pick up just from a having conversation with the surveyor before hiring them. Usually you can tell when someone is sleazy when you interact with them, so make sure that they have integrity

Arbitrator

Arbitrators work in alternative dispute resolution (or ADR). One method of resolving legal disputes outside of the courts and across international borders is through arbitration. Many people and corporations use it on a voluntary basis, so both parties have to agree to abide by any decisions that are made.

Role of an arbitrator in the construction process.

Unfortunately, many construction projects end in disagreements, which forces the parties to engage in some sort of conflict resolution. Many of these construction issues are settled by arbitration, which is a procedure by which the parties in disagreement agree to submit their case to a third-party neutral, known as the arbitrator, who serves as a judge and jury, rather than taking the matter to court. In essence, arbitration is a lawsuit that doesn't go to court.

The parties agree to submit their dispute to arbitration rather to filing a lawsuit in court (either in a contract before a dispute develops or by a later agreement to avoid a lawsuit). The parties' agreement gives the arbitrator the authority to rule on the rights and responsibilities of the parties, and that ruling will be enforceable against each party. Because the impartial third party renders a legally binding decision in arbitration, it differs significantly from mediation. However, arbitration and mediation are not incompatible. In many circumstances, parties will have a dispute resolution clause in their contract that will allow or compel the parties to mediate their disagreement first and then submit it to arbitration if the mediation is unsuccessful.
